American Time Usage

in the Era of the COVID-19 Pandemic


COVID-19

The pandemic radically affected millions of American's day-to-day lives. Jobs were lost, schools were closed, and travel was limited. We want to understand the magnitude of this disruption through analyzing the ways people spent their time differently during 2020.
Each year, the Bureau of Labor Statistics surveys roughly ten thousand households for the American Time Usage Survey, a project which tracks how Americans spend their time. This data helps track how our society chooses to use our collective time — on work, on leisure, on care, on volunteering. This also reveals how we prioritize or change certain behaviors during different time periods, like during a pandemic.

Time Usage Over the Last Two Decades

Consistently throughout the last two decades, the largest proportions of time are spent on work, education, and sleep. The overall breakdown of average time per activity is surprisingly steady.
Occasionally, there are bumps in how much time certain activities get, like a bump around 2014 in "government + civic activities" (which might be from voting in the midterm elections that year).

Overall, the relationship between income and hours worked is pretty flat — with actually the highest and lowest income earners working the most hours. As can be seen by the visualization above, the average number of hours worked are quite consistent over the last two decades, including during the 2020 pandemic.

Time Spent on Sleep or Personal Care

Younger people spend more time on sleep and personal care, with the median being between 10 and 12 hours per day. These age disparities really flatten out after the age of 20. Roughly 40% of people in the older age demographics spend an average of 8 to 10 hours on sleep and personal care.
